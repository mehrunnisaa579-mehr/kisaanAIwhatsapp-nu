# Mango Pest Management — Punjab / South Punjab

## Purpose

This file is for a FarmAI/KISAAN AI RAG knowledge base. It summarizes important mango insect pests and control actions for Punjab and South Punjab using the uploaded MRS Multan/AARI master directive as the primary source.

---

## 1. Mango Hopper

### Local names and farmer jargon

- Aam ka tela
- Lassi keera
- Hopper hamla
- تیلا
- آم کا چست تیلا

### Risk period

Mango hopper becomes aggressive in early spring, especially **February/March**, during flowering or boor emergence.

It hides inside:

- dark bark crevices
- dense canopy spaces
- shaded trunk areas

It often swarms tender panicles at night.

### Symptoms

- Small wedge-shaped greyish-brown insects
- Insects hop or fly when flowers are shaken
- Nymphs suck sap from floral panicles
- Blossom drying
- Flower drop
- Honeydew / sticky sweet secretion on leaves and flowers
- Sooty mold / black fungal layer on honeydew
- Canopy may appear charcoal black
- Photosynthesis is reduced when leaves are covered in sooty mold

### Why hopper is dangerous

Hopper attacks during flowering, so it directly affects:

- boor health
- fruit-set
- young fruit retention
- final yield

### Recommended chemical options from uploaded source

Spray timing:

- First spray: pre-flowering bud stage
- Second spray: after fruit-set, at mung-dana stage
- Avoid spraying during full bloom to protect honeybees

| Option | Product / Active ingredient | Dose |
|---|---|---|
| A | Imidacloprid 200 SL | **40–50 ml per 100 L water** |
| B | Bifenthrin 10% EC / Talstar | **60–80 ml per 100 L water** |
| C | Thiamethoxam 25% WG / Actara | **24 g per 100 L water** |

### FarmAI action rule

If the farmer reports full bloom and bees are active, avoid recommending routine insecticide spray unless hopper attack is severe. Protect pollinators.

## 2. Mango Mealybug

### Local names and farmer jargon

- Safaid thapa keera
- Mealybug
- Maloo keera
- میلی بگ
- سفید چور کیڑا

### Life cycle and timing

- Female mealybugs lay eggs in soil around the trunk base during summer.
- Newly hatched nymphs crawl up the trunk during **January and February**.
- They colonize spring vegetative flushes and fruit clusters.

### Symptoms

- White cottony or powdery insects
- Flat oval slow-moving colonies
- Clusters on tender shoots and fruit necks
- Sap sucking
- Young fruits shrivel
- Premature fruit drop
- Honeydew secretion
- Sooty mold growth on leaves and fruit

### Mechanical barrier rule

If the date is **December to January**, FarmAI should prioritize physical barrier control first.

Recommended barrier:

- Wrap a **12–18 inch wide** slick plastic sheet or band around the main trunk
- Place it around **3 feet above ground**
- Coat the band with grease
- This is called trapper band / plastic band
- It stops nymphs from crawling up the tree

This can reduce the need for later chemical sprays.

### Chemical options from uploaded source

Use only if insects have bypassed the barrier and reached the upper canopy.

| Option | Product / Active ingredient | Dose |
|---|---|---|
| A | Chlorpyrifos 40% EC | **200 ml per 100 L water** |
| B | Profenofos + Cypermethrin / Curacron | **150 ml per 100 L water** |

High pressure spray may be needed because the waxy coating protects the insect.

## 5. Short Farmer Advice Templates

### Hopper response

If small brown hopping insects are present on mango flowers and sticky honeydew or black sooty mold is visible, it may be mango hopper. Spray before flowering or after fruit-set, not during full bloom. Local options include Imidacloprid 200 SL at 40–50 ml per 100 L water, Bifenthrin 10% EC at 60–80 ml per 100 L, or Thiamethoxam 25% WG at 24 g per 100 L.

### Mealybug response

If white cotton-like insects are crawling on shoots or fruit clusters, it may be mango mealybug. In December–January, first use a 12–18 inch plastic/grease trunk band about 3 feet above ground to stop nymphs climbing. If insects have already reached the canopy, local options include Chlorpyrifos 40% EC at 200 ml per 100 L water or Profenofos + Cypermethrin at 150 ml per 100 L.
